Is my pet overweight?

Answer three quick hands-on questions to estimate your pet's Body Condition Score (1–9) — the same 1–9 scale vets use — and an ideal-weight target.

1. Feel your pet's ribs (sides of the chest). What do you notice?

2. Looking down from above, what's the body shape?

3. From the side, what does the belly line do?

Current weight (optional — for an ideal-weight target)

A guided estimate of the standard 1–9 Body Condition Score (ideal is 4–5). It's based on look-and-feel, so it's approximate — your vet can confirm with a hands-on exam. The ideal-weight target assumes roughly 10–15% over ideal per point above 5; use it as a goal, and make any weight change gradually under vet guidance.

Reading your pet's body condition score

This estimates whether your pet is underweight, ideal, or overweight — using the same 1–9 scale vets use.

What the numbers mean

  • BCS (Body Condition Score) — a 1–9 scale: 1 = far too thin, 9 = obese, and 4–5 = ideal.
  • The rib test — on an ideal pet you can feel the ribs easily with a light touch (like feeling the back of your hand) without pressing through fat.
  • Waist & tuck — from above, a healthy pet has a visible waist behind the ribs; from the side, the belly tucks up.

How to use it

Aim for a 4–5. Above that, trim treats and add gentle activity; below that, check with your vet.

🩺 Vet note: Big or sudden weight changes, or a score well outside 4–5, deserve a vet visit — and never crash-diet a pet.

Common questions

Why a 1–9 scale instead of a target weight?

Ideal weight varies hugely by breed, so body condition judges the individual pet.

My pet scored a 7 — how do I help?

Cut treats first, measure meals, add walks or play, and recheck monthly; your vet can set a safe plan.

Should I be able to feel the ribs?

Yes — easily, with a light touch. If you can't, there's too much fat over them.
