Your pet's age in human years

This converts your dog or cat's age into human years, using modern science instead of the old myth.

What the terms mean

  • The ×7 myth — multiplying by 7 is inaccurate: pets age fast early, then slow down.
  • The modern method — the first year ≈ 15 human years, the second adds about 9 more (≈24), then each later year adds roughly 4–5. Large dogs age faster than small ones.

How to use it

Use it for a fun but realistic sense of your pet's life stage — handy for knowing when senior care (more frequent checkups) makes sense.

🩺 Vet note: Large and giant breeds become senior earlier — ask your vet when to start senior screening.

Common questions

Why isn't it just ×7?

Pets mature very fast in year one, then slow down, so a flat ×7 gets the life stages wrong.

Do big and small dogs age the same?

No — large and giant breeds age faster and have shorter lifespans.

When is a pet a senior?

Roughly 7+ for many dogs (earlier for giant breeds) and about 10+ for cats; your vet can advise.

How pet years really work

The old “multiply by 7” rule is a myth. Pets age fast early in life, then slow down — and larger dogs age quicker than small ones. CanMyPet uses a more accurate model:

  • Year 1 ≈ 15 human years
  • Year 2 adds about 9 more (≈ 24 human years)
  • Each year after adds 4 (cats & small dogs) up to 7 (giant breeds)

That's why a 10-year-old Great Dane is "older" in human terms than a 10-year-old Chihuahua.
