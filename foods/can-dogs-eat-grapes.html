Can dogs eat grapes?

No — dogs should never eat grapes.

Grapes can cause sudden kidney failure in dogs, even in small amounts. The exact toxin is still unknown.

Why this verdict for dogs

Grapes can cause sudden kidney failure in dogs, even in small amounts. The exact toxin is still unknown.

Warning signs to watch for

  • Vomiting within hours
  • Lethargy
  • Reduced or no urination

⚠ If your dog ate grapes

Act immediately — don't wait for symptoms.

  • Call your vet or a pet poison helpline right away.
  • Note how much was eaten and when.
  • Do not induce vomiting unless a professional tells you to.

Grapes — and their dried forms, raisins and currants — are one of the few fruits that can be genuinely dangerous for dogs, and they don't follow the usual 'a little is fine' rule.

How much is safe?

There is no amount considered safe. Toxicity is unpredictable: some dogs eat several with no effect, while others develop kidney failure after just a few. Raisins, sultanas and currants are more concentrated and riskier gram-for-gram. Treat any amount as a potential emergency.

What happens if a dog eats too much?

Early signs usually appear within 6–24 hours: vomiting (often first), lethargy, loss of appetite and belly tenderness. As the kidneys are affected, increased thirst may be followed by reduced or no urination — a serious red flag.

⚠ What to do if your dog ate grapes

Call your vet or a poison line right away (ASPCA 888-426-4435 · Pet Poison Helpline 855-764-7661). If eaten very recently, the vet may induce vomiting and start IV fluids to protect the kidneys. Don't wait for symptoms.

Frequently asked questions

How many grapes are toxic to a dog?

There's no safe number. Because reactions are so unpredictable, even one or two can be a problem for some dogs — always treat ingestion as urgent.

Are raisins worse than grapes?

Yes. Raisins, sultanas and currants are dehydrated, so the toxin is more concentrated per piece — they're considered higher-risk.

My dog ate one grape and seems fine — should I worry?

Possibly. Many dogs are fine, but you can't predict which aren't. Call a poison line with your dog's weight for tailored advice.
