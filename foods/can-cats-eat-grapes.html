Can cats eat grapes?

No — cats should never eat grapes.

Out of caution, cats should never eat grapes — the same unpredictable kidney risk seen in dogs may apply.

Why this verdict for cats

Out of caution, cats should never eat grapes — the same unpredictable kidney risk seen in dogs may apply.

Warning signs to watch for

  • Vomiting
  • Lethargy

⚠ If your cat ate grapes

Act immediately — don't wait for symptoms.

  • Call your vet or a pet poison helpline right away.
  • Note how much was eaten and when.
  • Do not induce vomiting unless a professional tells you to.

Grapes — and their dried forms, raisins and currants — are among the few fruits that can be genuinely dangerous for pets, and they don't follow the usual 'a little is fine' rule.

How much is safe?

There is no amount considered safe. Toxicity is unpredictable: some pets eat several with no effect, while others develop kidney failure after just a few. Raisins, sultanas and currants are more concentrated and riskier gram-for-gram. Treat any amount as a potential emergency.

What happens if a cat eats too much?

Early signs usually appear within 6–24 hours: vomiting (often first), lethargy, loss of appetite and belly tenderness. As the kidneys are affected, increased thirst may be followed by reduced or no urination — a serious red flag.

⚠ What to do if your cat ate grapes

Call your vet or a poison line right away (ASPCA 888-426-4435 · Pet Poison Helpline 855-764-7661). If eaten very recently, the vet may induce vomiting and start IV fluids to protect the kidneys. Don't wait for symptoms.

Frequently asked questions

How many grapes are toxic?

There's no safe number. Because reactions are so unpredictable, even one or two can be a problem for some pets — always treat ingestion as urgent.

Are raisins worse than grapes?

Yes. Raisins, sultanas and currants are dehydrated, so the toxin is more concentrated per piece — they're considered higher-risk.

My pet ate one grape and seems fine — should I worry?

Possibly. Many pets are fine, but you can't predict which aren't. Call a poison line with your pet's weight for tailored advice.
