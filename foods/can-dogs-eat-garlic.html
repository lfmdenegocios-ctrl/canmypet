Can dogs eat garlic?

No — dogs should never eat garlic.

Garlic is part of the onion family and is several times more potent. It damages red blood cells.

Why this verdict for dogs

Garlic is part of the onion family and is several times more potent. It damages red blood cells.

Warning signs to watch for

  • Lethargy
  • Pale gums
  • Vomiting

⚠ If your dog ate garlic

Act immediately — don't wait for symptoms.

  • Call your vet or a pet poison helpline right away.
  • Note how much was eaten and when.
  • Do not induce vomiting unless a professional tells you to.

Garlic is in the onion (allium) family and carries the same blood-cell danger — but it's several times more potent, so it takes less to cause harm.

How much is safe?

Like onion, all forms are toxic and the effect adds up over time. Because garlic is so concentrated, even seasoning amounts in human food matter — garlic powder and garlic salt are especially risky.

What happens if a dog eats too much?

Watch for lethargy, weakness, pale gums, vomiting or diarrhea, and discolored urine — sometimes appearing a day or more after eating it.

⚠ What to do if your dog ate garlic

Call your vet or a poison line (ASPCA 888-426-4435 · Pet Poison Helpline 855-764-7661) with your pet's weight and the amount eaten.

Frequently asked questions

Isn't garlic good for fleas?

That's a popular myth. There's no reliable evidence garlic safely prevents fleas, and the amounts suggested can be toxic — use a vet-approved product instead.

Is a little garlic in cooking ok?

Best avoided. Garlic is potent and cumulative, so even seasoning-level amounts can add up — especially in small dogs and cats.

Is garlic worse than onion?

Gram for gram, yes — garlic is more concentrated, so smaller amounts can cause the same red-blood-cell damage.
