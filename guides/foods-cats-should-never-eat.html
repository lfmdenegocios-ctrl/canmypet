Cat Safety Guide

Foods Cats Should Never Eat

Cats are obligate carnivores with livers that handle some toxins even less well than dogs — so a "tiny taste" can matter more than you'd think. Worse, a few foods people assume are cat-friendly (hello, saucer of milk) are on this list. Here's what to keep away, how serious each is, the signs to watch, and what to do in an emergency.

⚠️ If your cat ate something dangerous — act now, don't wait for symptoms.
Call your vet or a pet poison helpline right away: ASPCA Animal Poison Control 888-426-4435 or Pet Poison Helpline 855-764-7661. Have your cat's weight, what and how much was eaten, and the time ready. Don't induce vomiting unless a professional tells you to.

The dangerous ones

Onion, garlic & chives Severe

The allium family damages a cat's red blood cells and causes anemia — and cats are more sensitive than dogs. Every form counts: raw, cooked, powdered, in baby food or broth.

Signs (often delayed a day+): weakness, pale gums, fast breathing.

Onion →   Garlic →

Chocolate & caffeine Severe

Theobromine and caffeine over-stimulate a cat's heart and nervous system. Cats rarely seek out sweets, but baking chocolate, coffee grounds and tea bags are real risks.

Signs: restlessness, vomiting, racing heart, tremors.

Chocolate →   Caffeine →

Grapes & raisins Severe

Linked to sudden kidney failure in pets, and the reaction is unpredictable — there's no known "safe" amount.

Signs: vomiting, lethargy, reduced or no urination.

Grapes →   Raisins →

Alcohol Severe

Toxic in tiny amounts for a small cat — and it hides in raw dough, desserts and unattended drinks.

Signs: wobbliness, drooling, slowed breathing.

Xylitol Severe

This sugar-free sweetener (gum, candy, some peanut butters) is dangerous for pets — keep anything "sugar-free" well out of reach.

Signs: sudden weakness, collapse.

Full guide →

Raw bread dough High

A double danger: it rises in the warm stomach (painful bloat) and the fermenting yeast produces alcohol.

Signs: swollen belly, retching, drooling, wobbliness.

Full guide →

Nutmeg High

Contains myristicin, which affects the nervous system in larger amounts — watch holiday baking and eggnog.

Signs: disorientation, racing heart, belly pain.

Full guide →

Salt & salty foods High

Chips, deli meat and jerky can push a small cat into sodium poisoning, which affects the brain and kidneys.

Signs: heavy thirst, vomiting, wobbliness.

🐟 The surprising ones — foods people think are fine

Milk & dairy Common myth

The classic saucer of milk is a myth — most adult cats are lactose intolerant, so milk and cheese cause stomach upset and diarrhea rather than a treat.

Signs: diarrhea, gas, upset stomach.

Milk →   Cheese →

Raw fish & too much tuna Risky habit

Raw fish contains an enzyme that destroys vitamin B1 (thiamine), and a steady diet of canned tuna can lead to deficiency and mercury concerns. An occasional bite of cooked fish is fine — a tuna habit isn't.

Signs of B1 deficiency: wobbliness, poor appetite, neurological signs.

Raw fish →   Tuna →

Too much liver In excess

A little cooked liver is fine, but a lot — over time — delivers too much vitamin A and can affect the bones and joints.

Signs: stiffness, lethargy over time.

Did you know? Cats can't taste sweetness — they lack the receptor for it. So their run-ins with sugary toxins like chocolate are usually about the fat and smell, not a sweet tooth.

Frequently asked questions

Can cats drink milk?

Most can't comfortably — adult cats are usually lactose intolerant, so milk causes stomach upset. Fresh water is all they need.

Is a little tuna okay?

An occasional bite of plain cooked fish is fine. The problem is tuna as a regular food — it isn't balanced for cats and raw fish can cause a vitamin B1 deficiency.

My cat licked something with onion in it — should I worry?

Possibly. Cats are very sensitive to alliums and signs can be delayed a day or more, so call a poison line with your cat's weight rather than waiting.

What's the first thing to do in an emergency?

Call your vet or a poison line (ASPCA 888-426-4435 / Pet Poison Helpline 855-764-7661) with the details, and don't induce vomiting unless told to. Our emergency tool walks you through it.
