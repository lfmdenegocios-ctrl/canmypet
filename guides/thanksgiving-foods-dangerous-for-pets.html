Holiday Safety Guide

Thanksgiving Foods: What's Safe & What's Dangerous for Pets

Thanksgiving is one of the busiest days of the year for pet poison hotlines — a dropped scrap or a "just this once" plate can turn into an emergency vet visit. The good news: a few things on the table are perfectly fine to share. Here's the simple safe-vs-dangerous breakdown so your pet can join the feast without the risk.

⚠️ If your pet sneaks something dangerous — act now, don't wait for symptoms.
Call your vet or a pet poison helpline: ASPCA Animal Poison Control 888-426-4435 or Pet Poison Helpline 855-764-7661. Have your pet's weight, what and how much was eaten, and the time ready. Don't induce vomiting unless told to.

✅ Safe to share (plain & in small amounts)

Plain turkey meat Safe

Skinless, boneless, fully cooked white meat is a great treat — no skin, no gravy, no seasoning, and absolutely no bones.

Serve: a few small unseasoned pieces.

Plain sweet potato & pumpkin Safe

Cooked and unsweetened, both are nutritious and gentle on the stomach — just skip the marshmallows, butter and pie filling.

Serve: a spoonful, plain.

Green beans & plain carrots Safe

Low-calorie, crunchy and dog-approved — as long as they're plain (no butter, salt or onion in the casserole).

Serve: a few bite-size pieces.

🚫 Keep off the plate

Cooked turkey bones Never

The single biggest Thanksgiving hazard — cooked bones splinter and can cause choking, mouth injuries or a punctured gut. Keep the carcass out of reach of counter-surfers and trash-divers.

Signs: gagging, drooling, retching, distress.

Full guide →

Onion, garlic & stuffing Never

Stuffing, gravy and green-bean casserole are usually loaded with onion and garlic — toxic to the whole allium-sensitive crew and damaging to red blood cells.

Signs (often delayed): weakness, pale gums, dark urine.

Onion →   Garlic →

Ham, gravy & fatty trimmings High risk

Rich, fatty, salty foods can trigger pancreatitis — a painful, sometimes serious inflammation that's a classic post-holiday emergency. Ham is also very salty.

Signs: vomiting, belly pain, hunched posture, no appetite.

Ham →   Gravy →

Chocolate & sugar-free desserts Never

Dessert season brings two big risks: chocolate (toxic to pets) and xylitol, the sugar-free sweetener in some baked goods and gum that's dangerous in tiny amounts.

Signs: vomiting, tremors, weakness, collapse.

Chocolate →   Xylitol →

Grapes, raisins & nutmeg Never

Hiding in fruit salad, stuffing and baked goods: grapes and raisins can cause kidney failure, and nutmeg (in pies and eggnog) affects the nervous system in larger amounts.

Signs: vomiting, lethargy, disorientation.

Grapes →   Raisins →   Nutmeg →

Alcohol & raw bread dough Never

Unattended drinks and rising dough are both dangerous — raw dough expands in the warm stomach and ferments into alcohol.

Signs: wobbliness, swollen belly, slowed breathing.

Alcohol →   Raw dough →
🐱 Cats too: the same hazards apply — and cats are especially sensitive to onion, garlic and bones. Keep the turkey carcass and trash secured, and see foods cats should never eat.
Did you know? The day after Thanksgiving is one of the busiest of the year for emergency vets — and pancreatitis from rich, fatty table scraps is one of the most common reasons. A few plain turkey bites are a far safer "treat" than a plate of leftovers.

Frequently asked questions

Can my dog have a little turkey?

Yes — plain, skinless, boneless, fully cooked turkey in small amounts is fine. Skip the skin, gravy, seasoning and bones.

What about a small piece of pumpkin pie?

Better not. Plain cooked pumpkin is great, but pie has sugar, spices and often nutmeg. Give plain pumpkin instead.

My dog grabbed a turkey bone — what do I do?

Call your vet or a poison line right away and watch for choking, gagging or distress. Don't try to pull a bone from the throat yourself.

How do I keep the day safe?

Feed your pet their normal meal first, tell guests not to share scraps, secure the trash and carcass, and keep a poison line number handy. Our emergency tool helps if something goes wrong.
